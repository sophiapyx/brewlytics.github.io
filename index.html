In 2020, the DailyHive reported that there were 715 food-and-beverage-based businesses, including 392 quick-service eateries and 135 full-service restaurants.

Yet in July 2025, the Vancouver Sun released an article talking about how a lack of customers is causing strain to the restaurant industry.

To understand what this looks like on a micro scale, we zoomed in on V6E, a dense and highly competitive area in the heart of Downtown Vancouver.

Looking at the current landscape by cuisine type.

One category clearly dominates: Japanese restaurants. In 2022, Vancouver Is Awesome even called Vancouver the “non-Japanese sushi capital of the world”.

Another notable category is cafés.

The Piccolo brothers, who have often been dubbed as the “Godfathers of coffee in Vancouver”, helped kickstart the Third Wave Coffee movement. Since then, specialty coffee has become a major part of the city’s identity.

There's also allegedly some drama with the Piccolo brothers but that is not my story to tell.

Yet sometimes, all good things have to come to an end…

This box plot shows the average lifespan of restaurants by cuisine. From here, we noticed a surprising pattern:

Japanese restaurants are everywhere, but they tend to stay open for less time on average. Italian restaurants, although fewer, have longer lifespans.

So popularity doesn’t always equal survival?

Digging deeper, we noticed that several addresses had multiple restaurants open in the same space across different years. This suggests high turnover.

So what does this all mean?

To understand the bigger picture, we need to look at the intersection of culture, economics and shifting consumer behaviour.

There could be a few reasons as to why there are so many Japanese restaurants:

  • Vancouver has one of the largest Asian populations outside of Asia
  • Access to fresh fish
  • The invention of the California Roll
  • Fusion dishes (sushi tacos, sushi pizza)

Cultural demand makes Japanese restaurants open frequently, but that doesn’t always ensure they stay open.

Economics is another factor. Japanese restaurants saturate the area, creating competition. Contrast this with Italian restaurants, which face less competition and survive longer.

Restaurant rent in Vancouver can range from $3,500 to $15,000 per month. Cioppino’s closed in 2024 when rent hit $60,000.

  • Rent
  • Food and ingredients
  • Utilities
  • Labour

Restaurants face increasingly tight margins. Combined with heavy competition, these pressures contribute to turnover.

Consumer behaviour has shifted. Chef J-C Poirier notes:

  • Staff shortages force reduced hours
  • Downtown foot traffic has dropped
  • Guests prefer affordable, fast options
  • Dining out is the first expense cut

Social media builds hype, but novelty wears off quickly.

The lifespan of restaurants is shaped by three key forces:

  • Culture influences what types open
  • Economics determines who survives
  • Consumer habits have changed

Restaurants open, close, and reopen. It is a dynamic, competitive race.

Popular cuisines appear quickly but also churn quickly. Less saturated categories are more likely to remain open.
