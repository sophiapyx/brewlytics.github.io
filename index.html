An Overview of Restaurant Industry in Vancouver

Scroll down to continue ↓

In 2020, the DailyHive reported that there were 715 food-and-beverage-based businesses, including 392 quick-service eateries, 135 full-service and sit-down restaurants, 110 retailer dealers, and 78 liquor primary businesses.

Yet in July 2025, the Vancouver Sun released an article talking about how a lack of customers is causing strain to the restaurant industry, which in turn affects 27 other sectors.

To understand what this looks like on a micro scale, we zoomed in on V6E, a dense and highly competitive area in the heart of Downtown Vancouver.

Using our dataset, we identified all the restaurants that are currently open. Here’s where they are located on the map.

This gives us a sense of how packed the neighbourhood is, and it also shows the competitiveness of the food industry here.

Looking at the current landscape by cuisine type.

One category clearly dominates: Japanese restaurants. In 2022, Vancouver Is Awesome even called Vancouver the “non-Japanese sushi capital of the world”. So, the large presence of sushi spots isn’t surprising.

(map showing active Japanese restaurants)

Another notable category is cafés.

(map showing active cafes)

The Piccolo brothers, who have often been dubbed as the “Godfathers of coffee in Vancouver”, opened 49th Parallel Coffee in 2004, helped kickstart the Third Wave Coffee movement, which focuses on the nuanced flavor of the beans themselves and where coffee is meticulously prepared and served in quality glassware or ceramics and highlights single origin coffees through pour overs and classic espresso beverages. Since then, specialty coffee has become a major part of the city’s identity, and that is visible in our dataset as well.

There's also allegedly some drama with the Piccolo brothers but that is not my story to tell.

Yet sometimes, all good things have to come to an end…

This box plot shows the average lifespan of restaurants by cuisine. From here, we noticed a surprising pattern:

Japanese restaurants are everywhere, but they tend to stay open for less time on average, while something like an Italian restaurants, although there are fewer restaurants, actually have longer lifespans

Even cafés, despite their cultural significance, don’t seem last as long as the Italian establishments too

So popularity doesn’t always equal survival?

Digging deeper, we noticed that several addresses had multiple restaurants open in the same space across different years. This suggests a high level of turnover, restaurants closing, new ones taking over the same units, and the cycle repeating. The neighbourhood constantly reshapes itself, reflecting changing trends, competition, and business pressures.

(Show map of overlaps)

So what does this all mean? To understand the bigger picture, we need to look at the intersection of culture, economics and shifting consumer behaviour

There could be a few reasons as to why there are so many Japanese restaurants:
    Vancouver has one of the largest Asian populations outside of Asia
    The fact that the ocean gives us access to fresh fish
    The fact that Vancouver created the California Roll
    The large immigrant population also leads to fusion dishes such as sushi taco, sushi pizza, etc.

Cultural demand makes Japanese restaurants open frequently, but that doesn’t always ensure they stay open.

Economics can also be another factor. The fact is, there are so many Japanese restaurants that are evidently saturating the area, creating more competition, potentially leading to shorter lifespans. Meanwhile the stark contrast when it comes to how many Italian restaurants means they face less competition, and are therefore, more likely to survive Oversupply seems to shorten lifespans, while niche, differentiated restaurants tend to last longer. Ultimately being popular doesn’t always guarantee survival!

Economic pressures extend far beyond competition. Restaurant rent in Vancouver can range from $3,500 to $15,000 per month, and in some cases, much higher. For example, Cioppino’s Mediterranean Grill in Yaletown closed in 2024 after 25 years when its monthly rent jumped to $60,000. With rising costs in:

    Rent
    food and ingredients
    Utilities
    labour

…restaurants face increasingly tight margins. Combined with heavy competition, these pressures can contribute to frequent turnover in such areas.

Consumer behaviour has also shifted dramatically.According to Michelin-starred chef J-C Poirier and Chambar co-owner Kerri Green:
    There’s a shortage of staff, forcing restaurants to reduce hours
    Downtown foot traffic has dropped: “There’s no one downtown… People aren’t going out after work”
    Guests now prefer affordable and fast options
    When people’s personal budgets shrink, dining out is often the first expense people cut

Social media and Michelin listings, built hype to boost the restaurant’s popularity, but that the novelty has worn off, with Poirier saying “There was excitement at first, but now people care less.” He adds: “In Europe, people embrace that dining experience. Here, people want good food and good value, without being overly challenged.” These shifting habits add yet another layer to the challenges restaurants face today.

So what does this all mean? The lifespan of restaurants are shaped by three key forces working together:
    Culture influences what types of restaurants open
    Economics determines which ones can survive
    Consumer habits have changed and not for the better

So is it because certain cuisines are more popular than others or social media hypes up a specific new restaurant? Maybe, but also looking at how the three factors interact together in a neighbourhood that is constantly shifting and reinventing itself is crucial to its success.

Restaurants open, close, and reopen under new names, not because the neighbourhood is failing, but because it is dynamic, competitive, and continually evolving. Our data shows:
    Popular cuisines appear quickly but also churn quickly
    Less saturated categories are more likely to remain open
    The neighbourhood’s design naturally encourages experimentation and renewal

In many ways, our data reflects Vancouver’s food culture as a whole: diverse, fast-moving, culturally driven, economically pressured, and always hungry for something new.
